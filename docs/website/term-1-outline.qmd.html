1 Year 9 Biology — Lesson Sequence

1.1 Weeks 1–2: Ecosystems, Populations and Ecological Monitoring

Curriculum focus: Population size and species diversity can be affected by abiotic and biotic factors; sampling techniques can be used to monitor abiotic factors and estimate numbers of organisms; ecological monitoring can be used to inform ecosystem health and impacts of human activity.

Lesson Topic Key Concept
1 Introduction to Ecosystems An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with each other and their non-living environment.
2 Abiotic and Biotic Factors Population size and species diversity are influenced by abiotic factors (temperature, light, water, soil pH) and biotic factors (predation, competition, disease).
3 Measuring Abiotic Factors Instruments such as thermometers, pH meters, light meters and moisture probes can be used to monitor abiotic conditions in an ecosystem.
4 Sampling Techniques — Quadrats Quadrat sampling allows scientists to estimate the number and distribution of organisms in a defined area.
5 Sampling Techniques — Transects and Capture-Recapture Transect lines reveal how species distribution changes across an environmental gradient, and capture-recapture methods estimate mobile animal populations.
6 Species Diversity and Abundance Species diversity is a measure of the variety and relative abundance of species in an ecosystem and is an indicator of ecosystem health.
7 Ecological Monitoring and Ecosystem Health Long-term ecological monitoring programs collect data that can be used to assess ecosystem health and detect the impacts of human activity.
8 Practical Investigation — Field Sampling Applying sampling techniques in a local ecosystem to collect data on abiotic factors and estimate organism abundance.

1.2 Weeks 3–4: Adaptations for Survival

Curriculum focus: Plants and animals have structural, behavioural and physiological adaptations that enable their survival in their environment.

Lesson Topic Key Concept
9 Introduction to Adaptations An adaptation is an inherited characteristic that improves an organism’s ability to survive and reproduce in its environment.
10 Types of Adaptations Adaptations can be classified as structural (physical features), behavioural (actions) or physiological (internal body processes).
11 Plant Adaptations to Local and South-West WA Ecosystems Plants in local and south-west Western Australian ecosystems have adaptations such as deep root systems, sclerophyllous leaves and proteoid roots to cope with nutrient-poor soils and dry summers.
12 Plant Adaptations to Deserts and Mangroves Desert plants conserve water through reduced leaves, thick cuticles and water-storing tissues, while mangrove plants have aerial roots and salt-excreting glands to survive in saline, waterlogged conditions.
13 Plant Adaptations to Bushfire Many Australian plants have fire-adaptive traits such as serotinous cones, lignotubers, thick bark and epicormic buds that allow them to survive or regenerate after bushfire.
14 Vertebrate Adaptations to Local and Desert Ecosystems Vertebrates in local and desert ecosystems display adaptations such as nocturnal behaviour, concentrated urine production and large ears for heat dissipation.
15 Vertebrate Adaptations to Polar and Marine Ecosystems Polar vertebrates have insulating blubber, countercurrent heat exchange and huddling behaviour, while marine vertebrates have streamlined bodies and specialised respiratory adaptations.
16 Comparing Adaptations — Research and Presentation Comparing the structural, behavioural and physiological adaptations of organisms from contrasting ecosystems to explain how each adaptation improves survival.

1.3 Weeks 5–6: Responding to Environmental Change

Curriculum focus: Organisms have mechanisms to respond to changes in their environment; endotherms and ectotherms respond differently to changes in external temperature; tropisms help plants respond to external stimuli.

Lesson Topic Key Concept
17 Responding to the Environment All organisms must detect and respond to changes in their environment in order to maintain conditions suitable for survival.
18 Endotherms and Ectotherms Endotherms (mammals, birds) generate internal body heat to maintain a stable temperature, while ectotherms (reptiles, fish) rely on external heat sources.
19 How Endotherms Regulate Temperature Endotherms use physiological mechanisms such as vasodilation, vasoconstriction, sweating, shivering and behavioural responses to regulate body temperature.
20 How Ectotherms Regulate Temperature Ectotherms use behavioural strategies such as basking, burrowing and changing body orientation, along with physiological colour changes, to absorb or avoid heat.
21 Comparing Heat Transfer in Endotherms and Ectotherms Both endotherms and ectotherms use conduction, convection, radiation and evaporation to transfer heat, but differ in the degree to which they rely on internal versus external mechanisms.
22 Plant Tropisms — Phototropism and Geotropism Tropisms are directional growth responses to stimuli; phototropism is growth toward or away from light, and geotropism is growth in response to gravity, both mediated by the hormone auxin.
23 Hydrotropism and Other Plant Responses Hydrotropism directs root growth toward moisture, and plants also respond to touch (thigmotropism) and other environmental cues to optimise resource acquisition.
24 Review and Consolidation Synthesising knowledge of adaptations, thermoregulation and tropisms to explain how organisms survive in changing environments.
